Data & methods

NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) is a satellite index of how much live green vegetation is present: it compares reflected near-infrared (strong from leaves) to red light (often absorbed by chlorophyll). Values are usually shown from about 0 (little or no green vegetation) up to near 1 (dense, healthy canopy). It is useful for comparing "how green" a place is across time and across regions, as well as for monitoring changes in vegetation health over time.

Our monthly composites use MODIS Terra L3 NDVI Monthly from NASA GIBS WMS (Aqua is used only for April 2025 when Terra has no tile). Pixels are decoded with the official GIBS NDVI colormap to produce approximate regional means.

How to read the NDVI map colors

The pixelated maps use NASA's GIBS NDVI palette: tan and brown colors represent low NDVI (little green vegetation or stressed/bare areas), yellow-green colors represent moderate NDVI, and darker greens represent high NDVI (dense, healthier vegetation).

Write-up

Our motivation was to make satellite vegetation data easier to interpret as a question of timing and place: which regions look stressed, which look healthy, and which months differ from what that region normally looks like during the same season? Raw NDVI maps can show where vegetation is greener, but they do not automatically tell a reader whether a month is unusual or simply following a normal seasonal cycle. We used NASA MODIS monthly NDVI imagery because NDVI turns reflected satellite light into a simple vegetation health measure. Higher NDVI generally means denser green vegetation, while lower NDVI can correspond to sparse vegetation, bare ground, or other vegetation stress.

We transformed the GIBS map tiles into regional summaries by decoding pixels through NASA's GIBS NDVI colormap, then aggregating each region-month into mean NDVI, median NDVI, stress share, and high-health share. Stress share is the percent of vegetated pixels below NDVI 0.30, while high-health share is the percent at or above NDVI 0.60. We also computed a same month historical baseline from prior years. This baseline is important because a region can look brown in winter without being unusually stressed. Comparing February to past Februaries separates expected seasonality from more unusual vegetation conditions.

The main map keeps the original spatial texture of the satellite data, while the summary tiles and stacked bar translate the map into comparable numbers. We used the GIBS NDVI palette for the maps because it is the source palette used to decode the pixels, then added a visible legend so readers can interpret tan/brown as low NDVI, yellow-green as moderate NDVI, and dark green as high NDVI. For the charts and stress bars, we avoided relying on red-green contrast by using orange for stress, gray for moderate vegetation, and teal/blue for healthier vegetation and NDVI lines.

We were careful not to make the month scrubber the only interaction. A slider by itself can be weaker than a static small-multiple chart because it hides previous months from view. To avoid that, the selected month is linked to the map, the stress/high-health bar, the full year NDVI line chart, the same month baseline, and a takeaway inside the stress panel. The chart keeps the whole year visible while the map shows spatial detail for the selected month, so users can move between overview and detail without losing context. In comparison mode, the same month and year control two regions at once, making regional differences easier to evaluate than if each map were explored separately.

We considered several alternatives before settling on this design. As mentioned in lecture, we considered showing twelve static maps for every region, but despite ease of comparison, that would take a lot of space and make it harder to connect each map to exact stress values and historical context. We considered pie charts for vegetation health categories, but stacked bars make percentage comparisons easier. For regional comparison, we considered overlaying two regions on one map, but side-by-side maps kept the spatial patterns readable. Overall, we chose a coordinated map, chart, baseline, and stress panel summary because it keeps spatial detail, numeric summaries, and historical context in sync.

Our development process was collaborative. For the checkpoint, each of us created two exploratory visualizations to help investigate our research question and better understand the NDVI data before designing the final interactive visualization. Elyse mainly focused on building the base of the interactive visualization by collecting and organizing the NDVI data from the past 20 years and creating the first rendered version for the checkpoint submission. Kyle helped develop the stacked bar chart for vegetation health comparison and later worked on the side-by-side comparison mode for viewing two regions at once. Philip spent a lot of time debugging the checkpoint version and later added the play feature along with additional summary statistic charts to improve the temporal exploration. However, we all collaborated with each other on our respective parts to create the final product.

Overall, we estimate that we spent around 10–20 people-hours developing the application. We did not strictly track our time. The parts that took the longest were making sure all of the interactive components updated correctly together and debugging issues with the comparison mode and playback features.
