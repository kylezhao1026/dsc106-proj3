Data & methods

NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) is a satellite index of how much live green vegetation is present: it compares reflected near-infrared (strong from leaves) to red light (often absorbed by chlorophyll). Values are usually shown from about 0 (little or no green vegetation) up to near 1 (dense, healthy canopy). It is useful for comparing “how green” a place is across time and across regions, as well as for monitoring changes in vegetation health over time.

Our monthly composites use MODIS Terra L3 NDVI Monthly from NASA GIBS WMS (Aqua is used only for April 2025 when Terra has no tile). Pixels are decoded with the official GIBS NDVI colormap to produce approximate regional means.

Between the listed regions, we see a mix of different climates and land across the U.S. with one tropical rainforest as a contrast. We use the following metrics to analyze the health of vegetation:

  • Regions: California Central Valley, Pacific Northwest, Great Plains, Southeast US, Amazon Basin.
  • Stress share: share of vegetated pixels with NDVI below 0.30.
  • High-health share: share with NDVI at or above 0.60.

Write-up

I chose my visual encodings and interaction techniques to make changes in vegetation health easy to compare across regions and over time. The NDVI maps use the MODIS color palette because it provides a clear gradient between low and high vegetation health, allowing users to quickly identify areas of stress and healthy growth. We also made it possible to zoom and pan on each map so people can magnify smaller areas and analyze specific sub-regions. I paired the maps with summary statistic tiles and stacked percentage bars so users could understand both the spatial distribution and the overall regional trends simultaneously. For temporal analysis, I used interactive line charts with hover tooltips, scrubbers, and playback controls so users could explore monthly NDVI patterns and compare them against historical baselines in a more dynamic and intuitive way. The month scrubber and playback controls make seasonal trends much easier to observe because users can watch vegetation conditions transition month by month instead of mentally comparing separate static images. Hover tooltips on the NDVI charts also allow readers to quickly inspect exact values and compare them against historical baselines without cluttering the visualization with too many labels. I also kept color blindness in mind and avoided a red–green color scheme for the charts and stacked percentage bars, choosing a more color-blind-friendly alternative (for example orange for stress and teal/blue for healthy) alongside the NASA palette on the maps.

I considered several alternative designs before arriving at these choices. For example, I thought about using only a slider for month selection, but that would not feel significantly different from viewing 12 separate static charts. The play bar instead allows users to observe month-to-month transitions and better understand seasonal progression over time. I also considered pie charts for displaying vegetation health categories, but stacked bars made percentage comparisons more accurate and easier to interpret. For the comparison view, I explored placing both regions on a single map, but side-by-side maps reduced visual clutter and made regional differences more noticeable. Overall, I selected these encodings and interactions because they balanced clarity, comparison, and ease of exploration while helping users connect spatial patterns with long-term vegetation trends.

Our development process was very collaborative. At the start of the project, each of us created two exploratory visualizations to help investigate our research question and better understand the NDVI data before designing the final interactive visualization. Kyle mainly focused on building the base of the interactive visualization by collecting and organizing the NDVI data from the past 20 years and creating the first rendered version for the checkpoint submission. Philip helped develop the stacked bar chart for vegetation health comparison and later worked on the side-by-side comparison mode for viewing two regions at once. Elyse spent a lot of time debugging the checkpoint version and later added the play feature along with additional summary statistic charts to improve the temporal exploration.

Overall, we estimate that we spent around 10–20 people-hours developing the application. We did not strictly track our time since a lot of the coding and debugging was done together in person. The parts that took the longest were making sure all of the interactive components updated correctly together and debugging issues with the comparison mode and playback features.

Interactive visualization

Normalized Difference Vegetation Index, abbreviated to NDVI, is a metric used to measure and map the health, density, and greenness of vegetation. Values range from -1 to 1, with higher values indicating denser, healthier vegetation.
Using map tiles colorized by NASA’s GIBS (Global Imagery Browse Services) palette, you can see where and when vegetation appears healthier! Choose a region and a year, or compare two regions that year, and visualize the mean greenness, stress share, and high-health share across the year!
